# Two hidden layers compute the maximum of six numbers

**Abstract.** We prove that `max(x1, ..., x6)` is exactly computable by a ReLU neural network with two
hidden layers (and arbitrary width). This resolves an open problem of Bakaev, Brunck, Hertrich, Stade,
and Yehudayoff [BBHSY], who established the analogous statement for five inputs and explicitly left the
six-input case open. We give an explicit construction as a signed combination of six S6-orbits of P2
support functions with denominator 360, and a fully exact, floating-point-free computer-assisted proof
of correctness. We also report computational evidence that six is the exact threshold: `max7` and `max8`
are not expressible in the same family even after substantially enlarging it.

## 1. Introduction

A feedforward ReLU network with `k` hidden layers computes a continuous piecewise-linear (CPWL)
function, and conversely every CPWL function is computed by some ReLU network [ABMM]. A central question
is how the number of hidden layers limits which functions are representable. The canonical test function
is the maximum, `maxn(x) = max(x1, ..., xn)`. A balanced binary tree of pairwise maxima computes `maxn`
with `ceil(log2 n)` hidden layers, and Hertrich, Basu, Di Summa, and Skutella [HBDS] conjectured this is
optimal: that exactly representing `maxn` requires `ceil(log2(n+1))` hidden layers, which would make the
ReLU depth hierarchy strict.

The conjecture holds for networks with integer weights: Haase, Hertrich, and Loho [HHL] proved an
integer-weight lower bound of `ceil(log2 n)` hidden layers via lattice-polytope volumes. Over the
rationals the picture is different. Bakaev, Brunck, Hertrich, Stade, and Yehudayoff [BBHSY] showed that
`max5` needs only two hidden layers, not the three the conjecture predicts, disproving the `n = 5` case
and improving the general upper bound. Their method subdivides the simplex into pieces realizable in two
layers. They left open whether two hidden layers suffice for six inputs.

**Theorem.** `max(x1, ..., x6)` is computable by a ReLU network with two hidden layers.

The construction uses rational weights, as it must: the integer-weight lower bound [HHL] would otherwise
force three layers. Our proof is computer-assisted but exact -- every arithmetic step is over the
integers or rationals, and no floating-point computation enters the certificate.

## 2. Preliminaries

**Support functions and the depth framework.** For a polytope `P`, its support function is
`h_P(x) = max_{g in P} <g, x>`. Then `maxn = h_{Delta}` for the standard simplex
`Delta = conv{e1, ..., en}`. Support functions are additive under Minkowski sum
(`h_{P+Q} = h_P + h_Q`) and satisfy `h_{conv(A u B)} = max(h_A, h_B)`.

Define polytope classes by `P0 =` points, and `P_{k+1} = { sum_i (P_i join Q_i) : P_i, Q_i in P_k }`,
where `join` is convex hull of union and the sum is Minkowski. Then `P1` is the zonotopes (Minkowski
sums of segments). A function is computable by a `k`-hidden-layer ReLU network iff it is a difference
`h_P - h_Q` with `P, Q in P_k` [BBHSY, building on HBDS]. Expanding the Minkowski sums and using
additivity, the two-hidden-layer functions are exactly the signed linear combinations of building blocks

    h_Q(x) = max_{g in Q} <g, x>,   Q = conv(Z1 u Z2) a join of two zonotopes.

So to compute `max6` in two layers it suffices to write `h_{Delta}` as a signed sum of such `h_Q`.

**The weight-2 lattice.** Following the subdivision approach, we take the building-block vertices on the
weight-2 lattice points of the dilated simplex `2 Delta^5`, namely `{2 e_i}` and `{e_i + e_j}` (21 points
in R^6). Zonotopes are generated by the difference vectors `e_a - e_b`, restricted so all vertices stay
on the lattice. This is the same family in which `max5` lives.

**Symmetry.** `max6` is invariant under the symmetric group `S6` permuting coordinates. Averaging any
construction over `S6` yields a symmetric one, so we search over `S6`-orbits of building blocks. This
collapses 16390 two-way joins to 78 orbit classes and makes an exact linear solve tractable.

## 3. The construction

Solving exactly for orbit coefficients reproducing `max6` and minimizing the number of terms yields a
construction with six orbit terms and denominator `D = 360`:

    max6 = sum over 6 S6-orbits  c_o * ( sum_{Q in orbit} h_Q ),
    c_o in { 1/360, -1/360, 1/360, 1/90, -1/90, -1/180 },   linear part 0.

The six orbit representatives (as vertex/gradient sets) are listed in `results/construction_max6.txt`.
Each `Q` is a join of two weight-2 zonotopes, hence each `h_Q` is a two-hidden-layer function, and the
signed sum is two hidden layers. Expanding the six orbits over `S6` gives 1530 individual `P2` terms.

## 4. The proof

We prove the displayed identity holds for all `x in R^6`. Both sides are CPWL, so equality is equivalent
to equality of gradients on every full-dimensional cell of their common arrangement.

**Reduction to a small arrangement.** On any region, the construction's gradient is
`sum_t c_t * argmax(Q_t)`, where `argmax(Q_t)` is the vertex of `Q_t` maximizing `<g, x>`. This selection
changes only across the hyperplanes `g - g' = 0` for `g, g'` vertices of a common term -- the "weight-2
difference" hyperplanes. By `S6`-symmetry it suffices to work in the closed fundamental chamber
`x1 >= ... >= x6`, where `max6 = x1` and the target gradient is `e1`. In gap coordinates
`y_k = x_k - x_{k+1} >= 0` (`k = 1..5`), a difference hyperplane with normal `h` becomes
`sum_k (sum_{i<=k} h_i) y_k = 0`; it is *forced* (constant sign on `y > 0`) unless its prefix sums change
sign. Of the 120 distinct difference hyperplanes, 85 are forced; only **35 are unforced**, giving an
arrangement of 35 hyperplanes in `R^5`.

**(1) Complete cell enumeration.** We enumerate every full-dimensional cell of the 35-hyperplane
arrangement in the open cone `y > 0` by a deterministic depth-first search over sign vectors, pruning
infeasible branches. There are exactly **2608 cells**, and for each we produce an exact integer interior
point, certifying that the cell is genuine.

**(2) Exact gradient.** At each cell's integer witness we compute, in exact integer arithmetic, the
construction's gradient `sum_t c_t * argmax(Q_t)`. On all 2608 cells it equals `e1`. (At an interior
point every relevant comparison is strict, so each `argmax` is unique and the gradient is well defined
and constant on the cell.)

**(3) Completeness by adjacency closure.** It remains to show the 2608 enumerated cells are *all* of
them, with no reliance on the floating-point pruning in step (1). The regions of a hyperplane arrangement
inside the connected cone `y > 0` form a connected graph under facet adjacency, and two adjacent regions
differ in exactly one hyperplane sign. For every enumerated cell and every one of its 35 single-sign
flips, the neighbor is either another enumerated cell, or empty. Emptiness is certified *exactly*: by
Gordan's theorem the open cell `{y : g . y > 0 for all g}` is empty iff there exist nonnegative rationals
`x`, not all zero, with `sum_j x_j g_j = 0` over the generators `g` (the orthant generators `e_k` and the
signed normals); we exhibit such an `x` and verify the identity over the rationals. Across all
`2608 * 35` flips, every neighbor is in-set (13756 flips) or exactly certified empty (77524 flips), with
zero uncertified. A nonempty subset of a connected graph that is closed under adjacency is the whole
graph, so the enumeration is complete.

By (1)-(3) the construction's gradient equals `e1` on every cell of the chamber, so the construction
equals `x1 = max6` throughout the chamber; by continuity and `S6`-symmetry it equals `max6` on all of
`R^6`. No floating-point arithmetic appears in (2) or (3). QED.

The certificate is reproduced by `prove_max6.py` and recorded in `results/proof_certificate.txt`. An
independent CPWL verifier (`verify.py`, a separate code path) agrees.

## 5. The frontier: six is (apparently) the threshold

The same machinery applied to larger `n` is sharply negative. For `n = 7` and `n = 8`, `maxn` is not in
the span of the weight-2 `P2` building blocks even after enlarging the family substantially (four-
generator zonotopes, three-way joins, vertex caps up to 20): the least-squares residual is bounded away
from zero (e.g. `~0.19` for `max7` against a rank-1481 basis), so no exact construction exists in the
family. By contrast `max5` and `max6` lie in this family. This locates a qualitative change exactly at
`n = 6`.

These negatives are *class-restricted* -- they bound the complexity of the `P2` pieces (zonotope size,
join arity) -- and so are evidence, not a lower bound. Still, the weight-2 family is the natural one for
two layers (its vertices are sums of two basis directions), and the consistency of the negative across
enlargements is striking. We therefore conjecture that six is the exact threshold: `maxn` is computable
in two hidden layers iff `n <= 6`.

## 6. Discussion

If the conjecture holds, `max7` becomes the natural candidate to witness a strict separation between two
and three hidden layers over the reals -- the central open question of this line, since no CPWL function
is currently known to require three hidden layers with unbounded width and real weights. The
`ceil(log2(n+1))` conjecture [HBDS] is now known to be false for `n = 5` and `n = 6`; whether its
lower-bound spirit survives for larger `n` is exactly what the `max7` question probes. Contemporary
lower bounds are consistent with our construction: [HHL] is integer-weight only, [AHM] is for
finite-precision weights, [GHL] is for braid-fan-conforming networks, and [Saf] is a width (not depth)
bound at depth at least three. None obstructs an exact rational two-hidden-layer construction for
`max6`.
